# Reading Digests

## What is a Reading Digest?

Every curated reading in a Prismatic Learning **course**, **arc**, or **path** comes with a **Reading Digest** — a 600–1,300 word long-form briefing that captures the source's argument, key passages, and the questions worth carrying into the text.

The digest is not a replacement for the original. It is a doorway. Read it first to orient yourself, then return to the source when you want to engage directly.

## What's inside a digest

A typical digest includes:

* **Setup** — Why this reading is in the course, what it's trying to answer, what came before.
* **Core argument** — The shape of the author's claim or the tradition's stance, in current English.
* **Anchor passages** — Two or three short excerpts that carry the most weight, with a sentence on why each matters.
* **Tensions and contradictions** — Where the reading disagrees with itself or with adjacent readings in the same course.
* **Prompts** — Two or three questions to hold while you read the source.

## Where to find them

* On a course page (`/courses/<course-slug>`), each reading card links to its digest.
* The course learn surface (`/courses/<course-slug>/learn`) lists every digest for the course in reading order.
* Reading Digests are also referenced from the [Seven Lenses](/wiki/parallax-engine) results when a tagged reading is cited.

## How to use them

1. **Open the digest before the source.** A few minutes here saves a lot of friction later.
2. **Note the prompts.** Carry them into the source instead of reading passively.
3. **Capture your own response.** Move what stays with you into the [Journal](/wiki/journal).
4. **Compare with the source.** Where does the digest sharpen the text? Where does it flatten it? That tension is part of the work.

## A note on authorship

Reading Digests are drafted with AI assistance and reviewed before they appear on a course. They are editorial, not authoritative — treat them like a knowledgeable teaching assistant's pre-class briefing, not the canonical reading. Notice where the digest is confident, where it hedges, and where it might be wrong.
